Break Your Selfie

Phtography by Marcus Smith

Is the selfie artistic expression or narcissistic expression? Hint: it might be time to break your selfie. Photographer XXX XXX, takes on our selfie culture and the results are all kinds of beautiful.

prev
1/4
next

Once smartphones came equipped with cameras that face backward to the user, social media seemingly transformed overnight, ushering in our glossy selfie culture.

Almost one-third of all photos taken by people aged 18-24: selfies.

The most re-tweeted message in history: selfie. Oxford Dictionary’s reigning Word of the Year: Yep, sel·fie.

While most every celebrity posts selfies to their social media account (we’re looking at you, a lot, Kylie Jenner, current title holder of the Celebrity with Most Instagram Selfies); it’s not just for the Kardashians, anymore. Dignitaries like First Lady Michelle Obama, Hillary Clinton and Pope Francis are in on the action, too.

93M

93 Million selfies are taken
every single day

So is everyone else. Currently, 93 million selfies are taken daily. It’s a statistic that bears repeating. Each and every day, 93 million times, someone purses their lips, invariably tilts their head downward roughly 11 degrees (see chart), and clicks, consciously intent on projecting a very specific image of their gorgeous, desirable life.

Sharing affirming selfies can drum up feelings of empowerment, but they can also reinforce society’s compulsion with appearances and its narcissistic embrace. At their worst, selfies can program us to obsess over our looks and judge ourselves on the basis of physical attractiveness rather than our accomplishments.

And not only can they skew how we see ourselves, but they can also change how our friends see us too. A recent psychological study suggests that the more selfies you post on social media, the less likeable you become. The UK researchers claim that posting too many selfies can alienate your friends, reduce the intimacy of your relationships and make others feel self-conscious about their looks in your presence, increasing feelings of competition.

Fortunately there’s a remedy for this selfie madness: Stop taking 93 million selfies every day.

It’s time to break our selfies. Or at least, challenge ourselves and our viewfinders to seek out less-plastic, more-human expressions of what it means to be beautiful.

It means embracing the gritty journey, not selling the glossy output.

It means auto-enhancing our lives not our j.pegs.

It means living our experiences will always take precedence over documenting them.

It means a life with fewer filters. Some visible blemishes, even scars. And no cropping out the gritty details. (That’s where the good stuff lives.)

It means no head-tilting, duck-facing or doe-eyeing and more fire, sweat and humility.

It means you’re at your damn sexiest when you’re pushing yourself to be your best.

It means capturing something real, honest.

Something more. Something human. Something more human.

Let’s see what you’ve got.

Grey Matters

An active body is an active mind

You want to be more quick-witted? Try salsa dancing. You want to improve your long-term memory? Start bench-pressing. You want a better brain? Simple. Lace up your trainers and start doing something. Just about anything. It turns out exercise may be our best defense against everything from mood orders to Alzheimers to addiction. And, more and more, science is showing that the harder we go after it, the greater the benefits may be.

1600 papers were published last year looking at exercise's effect on metal acuity.
The results: Lace up your runners.

Sixteen-hundred papers were published last year looking at exercise’s effect on mental acuity, and they all deliver the promise of an enlightened era in brain enlightenment. They conclude that exercise helps prevent cognitive decline, fends off cognitive disorders like ALS and Alzheimers and makes us better learners. We’ve learned that monkeys who run on treadmills learn twice as fast as sedentary simians. Sprinters learned vocabulary words 20% faster than non-sprinting exercisers. And dyslexic kids read up to 10% better after exercising.

In fact, the benefits of complex, high-intensity aerobic activities to cognitive function have become so embraced there are even CrossFit SAT-prep classes designed to help students improve their college entrance exams.

But this is just the tip of the brain stem when it comes to reasons your brain loves you when you work out. Studies show that exercise works in tandem with your brain to ease anxiety and enhance sex drive. It lowers your chances of getting diabetes. Unclogs your arteries. Improves your reaction time. And boosts your long-term memory.

More creativity. Increased productivity. Lifelong longevity. More and more studies report that these things are all achievable when exercise becomes a serious part of your life.

Exercise can even help reclaim self-control in the face of addiction. Smokers, trying to quit, who exercise regularly, are twice as likely to successfully put down the pack than those who don’t exercise.

A better workout can build a better brain.

Dr. John Ratey

Different types of physical activities can provide different and dramatic benefits to the brain. But now science shows that a proportional relationship exists between the intensity and complexity of our exercise and the amount of benefit to the brain. In essence, a better workout is building a better brain.

To help understand this dynamic, Associate Clinical Professor of Psychiatry at Harvard Medical School Dr. John Ratey provides this explanation. “To understand the far-reaching benefits of the brain, it’s helpful to understand some essential brain functions. One convenient model for the hierarchical organization of the motor system is a house with a basement and three floors. The basement holds our fixed actions (cerebellum). As we go up, each area contains increasingly more complex actions. In the middle floor, our more complex physical actions are governed (corpus striatum), all the way up to the command center (frontal cortex) responsible for our most complex functioning like planning, maintaining focus and response delay.”

“When exercising, benefits to the brain follow the same general guidelines. When we do simple low-intensity aerobic activities, those simpler basement level benefits come into play. But when we increase our aerobic intensity do more complex workouts that include high-intensity aerobics with elements of strategy and coordination, then we get a full continuum of benefits. From the most basic seratonin surges, all the way up to more complex concepts like building self-esteem and, while difficult to quantify, increasing our empathy for our team mates or community members.”

In essence, when you are playing at this level, you are on your way to bringing out your most human qualities.

  • About the Guardian

Congratulations, human. You are a bonafide Guardian. Guardians aren’t messing around when they use the expression “Treat others as you would like to be treated”. However, while plenty of people will go out of their way to help others, Guardians make a conscious decision not to neglect their own wellbeing in the process. Instead, they recognize that in order to help others reach their potential, they have to be pushing the boundaries for their own potential.

Even if that means failing from time to time. Guardians are nurturers by nature, people-oriented social animals who thrive on group-oriented activities. Living a long and healthy life is one thing. But living a long and healthy life surrounded by the tribe they’ve made their own is what Guardians strive for. It’s what makes them feel more human.

THE GUARDIAN’S APPROACH TO FITNESS

Living a healthy lifestyle is generally important to Guardians. Guardians may indulge in a few guilty pleasures from time to time or skip a workout when they’re not in the mood to exercise, but this doesn’t mean that they take their health for granted. Overall, Guardians appreciate their bodies and treat them well. They push hard to do their best with hopes of pushing others around them to do their best, too. That’s why it’s not uncommon to see Guardians doing their thing in workout groups or participating on teams in activities like charity runs meant to benefit others. The world needs humans like you, Guardian.
